### Protein of Interest: Human BRCA1 Protein


### General protein info 
**Uniprot ID**: P38398<br>
**Protein Name**: Breast cancer type 1 susceptibility protein<br>
**Length**: 1863 amino acids<br>
**Some Keywords**: activator, DNA-binding, cell cycle, DNA damage, transferase, DNA recombination, zinc, metal binding...<br>
**Function**: E3 ubiquitin-protein ligase that specifically mediates the formation of 'Lys-6'-linked polyubiquitin chains and plays a central role in DNA repair by facilitating cellular responses to DNA damage. Coordinates different cellular processes such as transcription regulator to maintaining genome stability.

### What domains are there?
1) Zinc-finger domain (N-terminal domain): The RING-type zinc finger domain interacts with BAP1.<br>
2) BRCT1/2 domains (C-terminal domain): recognize and bind phosphorylated pSXXF motif on proteins. The interaction with the phosphorylated pSXXF motif of ABRAXAS1, recruits BRCA1 at DNA damage sites.


### Gene ontology 

1) Molecular Function: DNA binding, enzyme binding, histone H2AK127 ubiquitin ligase activity, RNA binding, tubulin binding, zinc ion binding,...
2) Biological Processes: cellular response to mutagens (e.g. methanol, ionization, tumor necrosis factors) by inducing DNA damage response machinery. Some more specific processes involving this protein are fatty acid biosynthesis, angiogenesis, homologous recombination and regulation of gene expression via chromosomal CpG island methylation and even ss	random inactivation of X chromosome.


### Diseases and mutations?

As we have seen, BRCA1 protein is involved in many different cellular processes so if it were to mutate, it could lead to severe problems. One of the most known disease, involving multiple different BRCA1 mutations, is breast cancer. However, this mutation has also been seen in many other neoplasms, such as, ovarian cancer (5 mutated variants), prostate cancers, colon cancers and pancreatic cancers. Another disease harboring this mutation is Fanconi Anemia, but only 2 mutated variants have been annotated. 

### Sub-cellular location

On Uniprot, 6 publications reported BRCA1 in the nucleus, 3 publications in the chromosome and 1 publication found it in the cytoplasm.
The isoforms 3 and 5 were both found in the cytoplasm.
The localisation can be explained by the mechanism of action of BRCA1. It is recruited to DNA damage sites, and therefore mostly in the nucleus. However, during apoptosis, it can translocate to the cytoplasm.

### Post-translational modifications

The most common post-translational modifications are acetylation, isopeptide bonds, phosphorylation and Ubl conjugation.   
BRCA1 is phosphorylated in response to IR, UV and several stimuli that cause checkpoint activation. Phosphorylation at specific sites regulates mitotic spindle assembly or centrosomal microtubule nucleation.
BRCA1 can auto-ubiquitinate ('Lys-6'-linked polyubiquitination), which does not promote degradation, or be ubiquitinated in a FBXO44-dependent manner (proteasomal degradation).

### Isoforms

There are 8 isoforms produced by alternative splicing and alternative initiation. Additionally, there are 20 computationally mapped potential isoform sequences.  
Isoform 1 and isoform 3 are widely expressed, and isoform 3 is reduced or absent in several breast and ovarian cancer cell lines.
